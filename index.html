Notes & observations

May 2026 What I learned building Frontier Reflection

The idea behind Frontier was simple enough: instead of asking an AI to summarise a document, ask it to go and find everything relevant about an emerging market — macro conditions, political risk, sector opportunity, FX dynamics — and return something you could actually act on. A research brief, not a list of links.

What I didn't expect was that the hardest part wouldn't be the AI at all.

The AI was the easy part

LangGraph made the multi-agent orchestration surprisingly manageable. You define nodes, you define edges, you run them in parallel. The six research nodes — macro, political risk, sector, exit landscape, FX, market timing — all fire simultaneously and their outputs feed into a single synthesis call. Getting that working took less time than I expected.

Google Gemini handled the synthesis well. Tavily pulled live web data per node. The technical stack came together faster than the thinking behind it.

Making the output actually useful — that was the hard part

The first version returned a wall of text. Technically correct, sometimes impressive, almost entirely unusable. It answered the question asked but didn't answer the question behind it: what should I do with this?

The shift that mattered most was structural. Moving from a freeform synthesis to a rigidly templated brief — with named sections, a consistent hierarchy, chart data extracted alongside the prose — forced the model to organise its thinking in a way that was actually readable. The constraint made it better.

The second shift was the router. Simple or definitional queries don't need a 25-second pipeline. Adding a pre-classifier that answers those in 2–3 seconds changed how the whole system felt to use. Speed is a design decision, not just an engineering one.

What I'd do differently

Build the backend properly before touching the frontend. I got drawn into the D3 choropleth maps and Chart.js panels earlier than I should have — they're satisfying to build and they look good, but they created pressure to make the data fit the visualisation rather than the other way around. Backend first, always.

I'd also connect real data APIs from the start rather than using stubs. The stubs were fine for prototyping but they delayed the moment of reckoning: does this actually work on real, messy, inconsistent data? That question should come early, not late.

The thing that surprised me most

How much the quality of the output depends on the quality of the prompt architecture — not the model choice, not the framework, not the infrastructure. The model is the least interesting variable. What you ask it to do, in what order, with what constraints, and how you structure what comes back: that's where the real work is. That's what takes time to get right.

Frontier is on GitHub if you want to look at the code. The README explains the architecture in more detail than I've gone into here.

Apr 2026 What I've learned building agents at IBM Reflection

Before I built Frontier, I built agents at IBM. The tools were different — watsonx Orchestrate instead of LangGraph, enterprise constraints instead of a blank canvas — but the problems were surprisingly similar. And the most important thing I learned had nothing to do with the technology.

The demo is part of the product

At IBM, everything you build eventually goes in front of a client. I presented agents to senior stakeholders at major financial institutions and universities. These were not developers. They were not interested in architecture decisions or framework comparisons. They wanted to know one thing: does this solve a problem I actually have?

That question changes how you build. When you know a non-technical audience will be in the room, you stop optimising for technical elegance and start optimising for clarity. What does the agent say when it's working? What does it say when it isn't? How do you explain what it's doing without losing the room?

The ability to communicate AI clearly — to make it feel useful rather than impressive — is a skill that doesn't appear in any tutorial. You learn it by being in the room when it goes wrong, and figuring out how to recover.

Enterprise constraints are a design input, not an obstacle

Building inside IBM meant building inside guardrails. Approved tools, security requirements, stakeholder sign-off at each stage. Early on this felt like friction. Later it felt like useful pressure — it forced clarity about what the agent actually needed to do, rather than what was interesting to build.

The banking companion agent I built had to work within tight parameters. No hallucinations, predictable outputs, clear failure modes. Those constraints pushed me towards better prompt architecture and more careful output validation than I would have applied working alone. Some of the habits I developed there carried directly into Frontier.

The gap between demos and real problems

The agents I built at IBM were responding to real briefs from real clients — not invented problem statements from a course. That gap is larger than it sounds. Real problems are messier, less well-defined, and often not quite what the client said they were when you first heard them.

Learning to scope an agent properly — to ask what it needs to do, what it shouldn't do, and what happens at the edges — came from working with actual stakeholders, not from documentation.

What IBM didn't give me — and Frontier did

Scope. The agents I built at IBM were focused, contained, purposeful. Frontier was something different — six parallel research nodes, a streaming API, a full frontend, a synthesis layer that had to hold everything together. The complexity was an order of magnitude higher, and there was no framework telling me what to do next.

IBM taught me how to build agents that land with real people. Frontier taught me how to build a system. Both turned out to be necessary.
